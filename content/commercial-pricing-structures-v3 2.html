1 Synthesize — Typical Price/Margin % and Discount %/Multiplier

A percentile-based machine learning model finds the typical price from historical transaction patterns, replacing rigid reference points with data-driven baselines. Each dot represents a customer.

How it Works

List Price Model — A percentile-based model sets the typical list price for each product using product attributes (cost basis, category, competitive positioning, volume history). It finds where list prices naturally cluster across transactions, replacing rigid reference prices with data-driven baselines.

Granularity: per product or SKU family. Typically 8–15 product attributes.

Discount Model — A second percentile-based model estimates the typical discount or multiplier each customer should receive, based on customer attributes (revenue size, industry, purchase frequency, wallet share, tenure). The model finds what "like-for-like" customers typically pay.

Granularity: per customer. Typically 10–20 customer attributes.

The ML baseline reflects what has happened. Steering adjusts it toward what should happen — through strategic overrides and automated rules that fire on external signals.

How it Works

Strategic overrides are informed by data analysis, market intelligence, and domain expertise. Pricing managers review the synthesized prices and adjust based on go-forward strategy — e.g., raising list prices on supply-constrained products, protecting margins on strategic accounts, or correcting discounts built on distorted negotiation history.

Automated rules encode recurring business logic that runs after synthesis. List price rules respond to signals like inventory tightness, input cost changes, and market benchmarks. Discount rules adjust for customer-level factors like growth targets, spend trajectory, over-discounted history, and churn risk.

3 Experiment — Alternative Price

Test specific price changes on subsets of customers to measure causal impact on win rates and revenue.

How it Works

Controlled tests measure the causal impact of price changes before rolling them out broadly. A subset of customers or products is randomly assigned a different price (higher list, deeper discount, or both), and win rates, revenue, and retention are tracked against a holdout group at the current price.

This isolates the effect of the price change from other factors (seasonality, rep behavior, market shifts) — answering "what actually happens when we change this price?" rather than inferring it from historical correlation.

4 Optimize — Desired Outcome

Causal models estimate price elasticity and find the price that maximizes the desired business outcome.

How it Works

Causal econometric models estimate how sensitive each customer or product segment is to price changes — the price elasticity of raising a list price or deepening a discount. Using historical win/loss outcomes, deal attributes, and experiment results from Stage 3, the model estimates P(win) at each price point and finds the price that maximizes expected profit (win probability × margin).

Inputs: win/loss outcomes, price variation across deals, customer and product attributes. Output: optimal list price and discount per segment with floor, target, and stretch guidance.

1 Synthesize — Typical Price Level

Two models work together: one repositions product level boundaries where demand naturally breaks, the other estimates each customer's typical margin and assigns them to the closest level.

How it Works

Product Level Boundaries — A quantile-based model determines where price levels should sit for each product group. It analyzes historical transaction prices using product attributes (cost basis, category, competitive position, volume band) to find natural price breakpoints — where demand actually clusters rather than where policy assumes it does.

Granularity: per product group or SKU family. Typically 8–15 product attributes.

Customer Level Assignment — A second model estimates the typical weighted-average margin % each customer should achieve, based on customer attributes (revenue size, industry, tenure, wallet share, growth trajectory). The customer's expected basket margin is then calculated at each product level, and they're assigned to the level closest to their model estimate.

Granularity: per customer. Typically 10–20 customer attributes.

1 Synthesize — Typical Price/Margin %

Peer group models bucket deals by 3–5 attributes and apply global adders to compensate for thin groups. An ML model uses all attributes simultaneously — no buckets, no blunt adjustments. Each dot is a deal.

How it Works

Peer group target — Deals are bucketed by a handful of attributes (product family, customer tier, region). Within each bucket a percentile target is set. When a bucket is thin, a global adder shifts the entire target up or down to compensate — but the adder applies equally to every deal, over-correcting some while under-correcting others.

ML target — A gradient-boosted model considers 20–40 attributes simultaneously and learns local patterns without needing buckets or adders. The result is a target that fits each deal context individually.

Granularity: per deal or quote. Typically 20–40 attributes across product, customer, and order dimensions.

4 Optimize — Desired Outcome

With a continuous price variable, causal models directly estimate P(win|price) and find the price that maximizes expected profit.

How it Works

Causal price-response models estimate P(win | price) for each deal context using historical win/loss outcomes and the natural price variation across similar deals. With a continuous price variable, the model directly finds the price that maximizes expected profit — producing floor (minimum acceptable), target (profit-maximizing), and stretch (aspirational) recommendations.

Inputs: win/loss outcomes, quoted vs. transacted prices, customer and deal attributes. Output: floor, target, and stretch price per deal with confidence intervals.
